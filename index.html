What is Web Accessibility?

Web Accessibility is the practice of making your website easier to use for everyone, especially those with disabilities.

It includes considerations towards responsive design for all types of devices, proper use of HTML tags and their attributes, including functionality for assistive technologies and screen readers, and more.

Why does it matter?

Web Accessibility is important for several reasons:
  • It ensures that users with disabilities are more easily able to use your site.
  • It makes your site more accessible to all users on mobile devices or with slower connections.
  • It helps your site acheive higher rankings in search engines.
  • It reduces the risk of legal action resulting from a website that may be seen as discriminating against the disabled.

According to w3.org, "at least one billion people – 15% of the world’s population – have a recognized disability." This includes a variety of issues such as visual, auditory, and physical impairments among others. Imagine being unable to hear content on a media rich site that does not include captions or relying on a screen reader, which describes the content on a page, that is unable to decipher improperly formatted code. Users who experience such issues are likely to quickly leave your site. Keeping your site up to snuff with current accessibility standards encourages these users to stay and revisit your site.

This doesn't only affect the population living with a disibility however, as all users benefit from the design choices that create accessible websites. An accessible site that's been designed to look good and be easy to use on all devices will inevitably also work well on mobile devices like smartphones and tablets. According to Statista, as of 3rd quarter 2018, 52.4% of worldwide website traffic is done with a mobile device. That means a little over half of the people visiting your site will need it to function with ease even if they aren't on a traditional computer.

Your site will also benefit from increased traffic; not only do users who would have left your site due to inaccessibility stay and return, but most search engines rank accessible websites higher than others. Because of this, accessibility in design should be considered during Search Engine Optimization (SEO).

Additionally, the Americans with Disabilities Act (ADA), which states that “places of public accommodation” are accessible to the disabled, can be considered to be violated by a website that is difficult or impossible to be used by disabled users. In the past decade, both private litigants and the Department of Justice (DOJ) have initiated legal action against sites that are inaccessible with some success and the DOJ is soon expected to release guidelines specifically regarding websites. According to w3.org, "the Convention on the Rights of People with Disabilities (CRPD) is a comprehensive human rights document that includes a direct reference to the rights of all people to have equal access to communications technology. Passed by the General Assembly of the United Nations, more than 175 countries ratified it by 2018." As time passes the legal and financial risk of ignoring web accessibility grows.

In most cases, the design and implementation of web accessibility is quick and easy. The added costs are minimal, but the benefits of increased traffic, better user experience and reduced legal risk are substantial.
